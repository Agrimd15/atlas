Business Overview
Plain English
  • Marvell designs the specialized chips that move and process data inside AI data centers.
  • It does two things: it sells off-the-shelf connectivity chips, the optical and networking silicon that shuttle data between thousands of AI processors, and it co-designs custom AI accelerators for individual cloud giants like Amazon and Microsoft.
  • It does not run its own factories; TSMC builds the chips.
  • Marvell does not sell to consumers; its customers are the largest cloud and networking companies in the world.
The technical version
  • Marvell is a fabless designer of data-infrastructure silicon manufactured at TSMC. Its merchant portfolio includes PAM4 and coherent optical DSPs (Spica/Nova/Ara at 1.6T), silicon photonics, Teralynx Ethernet switch silicon (51.2 Tbps class), and Arm-based infrastructure CPUs.
  • Its custom-silicon business co-designs application-specific XPUs/ASICs and interconnect IP for individual hyperscalers (e.g., AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia), using in-house SerDes, advanced-packaging and IP. The March 2026 NVIDIA deal integrates Marvell custom XPUs and scale-up networking into NVLink Fusion.
  • FY2026 revenue ~74% data center; management targets ~$11.5B FY2027 and ~$16.5B FY2028 revenue with >$10B custom-silicon revenue by FY2029.
Explained simply
  • Think of an AI data center as a city of tens of thousands of AI brains that must talk to each other constantly.
  • Marvell makes two things that city needs.
  • First, the roads and fiber-optic highways, the chips that let all those brains exchange data at light speed without traffic jams.
  • Second, for the biggest cloud companies, Marvell will custom-build a brain designed for exactly that company's work, instead of an off-the-shelf one.
  • Broadcom is the biggest company doing this custom-brain work; Marvell is the clear number two.
  • And in early 2026, Nvidia, the company that makes the most popular AI brains, bought a piece of Marvell and teamed up with it, a strong vote of confidence.
  • Marvell (NASDAQ: MRVL) is a fabless data-infrastructure chipmaker that has re-centered itself on the AI data center.
  • FY2026 revenue was a record $8.195B, up 42% YoY, with data center ~74% of the total (~$6.1B).
  • It runs two engines: merchant connectivity silicon (PAM4/coherent optical DSPs, Teralynx switching, Arm CPUs) and custom silicon (XPUs/ASICs) co-designed for individual hyperscalers.
  • Custom silicon scaled from ~zero to roughly a quarter of data-center revenue across 18+ cloud design wins and 50+ active opportunities with 10+ customers.
  • It is the clear merchant #2 in custom AI silicon behind Broadcom, and a leader in high-speed electro-optics for AI interconnects.
  • In March 2026 NVIDIA took a ~$2B (~2.5%) stake and entered an NVLink Fusion / silicon-photonics / AI-RAN partnership.
  • Management targets ~$11.5B revenue in FY2027 and ~$16.5B in FY2028, with >$10B custom-silicon revenue by FY2029.
  • In June 2026 the stock re-rated sharply after NVIDIA's Jensen Huang publicly flagged Marvell as a potential trillion-dollar company.
  • It pruned non-core automotive by selling its Brightlane Automotive Ethernet unit to Infineon for $2.5B (closed Aug 2025).

Product & Revenue Model
  • Merchant data-infrastructure silicon, PAM4/coherent optical DSPs (Spica/Nova/Ara 1.6T), silicon photonics, Teralynx Ethernet switching, and Arm-based infrastructure CPUs, sold across many customers.
  • Custom silicon, bespoke XPUs/ASICs and interconnect IP co-designed for individual hyperscalers (AWS Trainium, Microsoft Maia), now ~a quarter of data-center revenue and the primary growth driver.
  • All fabricated at TSMC. The March 2026 NVIDIA partnership embeds Marvell custom XPUs and scale-up networking into NVLink Fusion.

Key Risks & Debates
  • Priced for acceleration: a steep EV/Rev multiple (see live comps) leaves the stock exposed to compression if the FY2027 ramp or custom-silicon design wins disappoint.
  • Competition: Broadcom (the #1) and Taiwanese ASIC houses (Alchip, GUC) are pressing on custom-silicon economics and design wins.
  • Margin mix: rising custom-silicon mix carries lower gross margin than merchant products, pressuring blended GM as the segment scales.
  • Customer concentration: data-center revenue is concentrated in a handful of hyperscaler custom programs (AWS, Microsoft); a single program slip is material.
  • NVIDIA dependence/optics: the NVLink Fusion partnership deepens reliance on NVIDIA's roadmap and raises questions on the economics of NVLink-linked designs.
  • Narrative sensitivity: the June 2026 'trillion-dollar' re-rating leaves a high bar; sentiment-driven moves cut both ways.
  • Cyclical tails: enterprise, carrier, and consumer remain off cyclical troughs and could relapse.
